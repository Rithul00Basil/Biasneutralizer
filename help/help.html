What the bias rating means

‚ñº

You'll understand how we calculate ratings and what each one means.

How ratings work

We analyze the language patterns in how a story is told, not the facts or your political views. Think of it like a grammar checker, but for bias instead of spelling.

Our 3-factor scoring system

  1. Loaded language count: Emotional or charged words
  2. Structural framing: How information is organized and emphasized
  3. Balance score: Whether multiple perspectives are included

These combine into evidence points that determine the final rating.

The 6 ratings explained

Strong Left 4+ evidence points

Multiple loaded phrases favoring progressive perspectives, limited conservative viewpoints

Example: "Republicans gutted healthcare protections" vs. "reduced"

Lean Left 2-3 evidence points

Moderate use of left-leaning framing or language patterns

Example: "Climate crisis" vs. "climate change" (when repeated)

Center 0-1 evidence points

Neutral language with balanced sourcing and minimal emotional framing

Example: "Lawmakers criticized" vs. "slammed" or "praised"

Lean Right 2-3 evidence points

Moderate use of right-leaning framing or language patterns

Example: "Tax relief" vs. "tax cuts" (when benefits emphasized)

Strong Right 4+ evidence points

Multiple loaded phrases favoring conservative perspectives, limited progressive viewpoints

Example: "Democrats hijacked the bill" vs. "amended"

Unclear Mixed signals

Conflicting evidence or insufficient content to determine clear direction

Example: Opinion pieces or very short articles

Important: A "Center" rating doesn't mean factually accurate. A biased rating doesn't mean factually wrong. We only analyze how the story is told, not what the story reports.
+ Deep dive: What counts as "loaded language"?

3 categories of bias indicators

üéØ Emotional Language

Words chosen to provoke emotional response rather than inform

‚ùå Slammed, destroyed, radical, hero ‚úÖ Criticized, reduced, progressive, supporter
üèóÔ∏è Structural Framing

How information is ordered and emphasized

  • Burying counterarguments at the end
  • Headline choice that primes interpretation
  • Quote selection that favors one side
üìä Source Selection

Which voices are included and excluded

  • Only quoting experts from one viewpoint
  • Omitting relevant opposing perspectives
  • Unequal time/space given to different sides

Two types of bias

In quotes (Source Bias)
Bias from people being quoted ‚Äî we note it but don't penalize articles for it.
In narration (Journalistic Bias)
Bias from the author's word choice and framing ‚Äî this is what we rate.
